Messaging patients

  • Communication channels for messages
  • Send accessible and inclusive messages
  • Send messages that patients trust
  • Send messages that are appropriate and relevant
Content guide -

Send messages that are personalised, appropriate and relevant

Different types of messages will need different levels of personalisation.

Think about the context of the messages you send.

Adapt the content and tone to make your messages appropriate for the situation.

Target your batch messages to specific groups

Think about who your message is relevant to. Use your messaging software to identify a group of patients that’s as specific as possible.

For example, a message might only be relevant to patients with diabetes who are on a certain medication.

Research insight

Engagement with messages falls if patients receive too many messages that are not relevant to them.

Consider different channels to reach wider groups

Use the information you know to make message content as relevant as possible to specific groups.

For example, you may have a group of patients have previously attended their screening appointments but missed their most recent one. You can send a message reminding them of the importance of regular screening.

Tailor your message content

Think about who your message is relevant to. Use your messaging software to identify a group of patients that’s as specific as possible.

Confidential content

Be cautious about what personal information you include in messages. The messages you send to a patient could be seen by other people. For example, many families will share a single mobile phone.

Some people also regularly change their mobile number, so it is important to confirm patients’ contact details at every opportunity.

Include enough information for the patient to understand what the message means and any action they need to take, without including any specific details of their care.

Consider how messages make patients feel

Messages should make patients feel cared for. You should try to avoid making patients feel anxious.

Follow the Voice and tone section of our Content guide to write messages that are:

  • personal, rather than formal
  • empowering to patients
  • trustworthy
