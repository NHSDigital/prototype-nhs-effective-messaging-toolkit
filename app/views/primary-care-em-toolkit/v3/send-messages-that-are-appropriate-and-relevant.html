Messaging patients

Content guide -

Send messages that are personalised, appropriate and relevant

Different types of messages will need different levels of personalisation.

Think about the context of the messages you send.

Adapt the content and tone to make your messages appropriate for the situation.

Send relevant messages to specific groups of people

Research has shown that engagement with messages falls if patients receive too many messages that are not relevant to them.

Identify specific groups and tailor your message content

Use your messaging software to identify groups of patients that are as specific as possible.

Then use information you have available to make the message content as relevant as possible to those specific groups.

Example

Example:

You may have identified a group of patients who you know have previously attended their screening appointments but missed their most recent one.

You can send a message reminding them of the importance of regular screening.

Consider different channels to reach wider groups

Do not send messages to very large groups of patients unless there is a real need to.

Consider whether general information can be shared in other ways, like putting a banner on your website or a post on social media.

Confidential content

Be cautious about what personal information you include in messages. The messages you send to a patient could be seen by other people. For example, many families will share a single mobile phone.

Some people also regularly change their mobile number, so it is important to confirm patientsâ€™ contact details at every opportunity.

Include enough information for the patient to understand what the message means and any action they need to take, without including any specific details of their care.

Consider how messages make patients feel

Messages should make patients feel cared for. You should try to avoid making patients feel anxious.

Follow the Voice and tone section of our Content guide to write messages that are:

  • personal, rather than formal
  • empowering to patients
  • trustworthy
