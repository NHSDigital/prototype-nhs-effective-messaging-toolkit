Messaging patients

  • Communication channels for messages
  • Send accessible messages

Send accessible and inclusive messages

Write and send messages that:

  • everyone can understand
  • make everyone feel included and respected

Make standard messages accessible

Aim to make all the text messages, emails and app messages you send accessible to people who:

  • use assistive technology, like a screenreader
  • have a low level of literacy
  • have low numeracy
  • have a learning difficulty or autism
  • have co-ordination difficulties
  • do not speak English as their first language

This will reduce the number of messages you need to send in alternative formats. These are also known as accessible formats.

Use our content guide to write messages that are:

  • clear and in in plain English, so someone with a reading age of 9 to 11 years can understand them
  • concise, with short sentences and paragraphs
  • in the active voice, not passive voice

Specific pages of the content guide tell you how to:

  • use words and phrases that patients understand
  • use formatting and punctuation
  • write numbers, measurements, dates and times
  • use links

The GOV.UK style guide also has a useful list of words to avoid, with plain English alternatives.

The messages you send might include links to your website. Find out how to make your website usable and accessible for patients.

How stress and trauma can affect understanding

People experiencing stress and trauma may not understand things the same way they usually would.

Research insight

People experiencing stress and trauma cancelled appointments they wanted to reschedule. They also thought a "referral" was the same as an "appointment".

Many people will be stressed or experiencing trauma when reading messages related to their healthcare. Messages need to:

  • be easy to scan
  • tell people exactly what they need to do
  • be as specific as possible, to reduce the chance of people misunderstanding

The Accessible Information Standard

All organisations that provide NHS care are [legally] required to follow the Accessible Information Standard. The standard outlines how you must meet the information and communication support needs of people with a disability, impairment or sensory loss. This includes patients and service users, and their carers and parents.

Who the Accessible Information Standard might apply to

You must ask anyone who uses your services and has a disability, impairment or sensory loss if they have any communication or information needs.

Patient codes that may indicate an additional communication need:

  • registered blind or partially sighted
  • hearing loss
  • hearing aid user
  • registered deaf
  • sensori-neural deafness
  • autistic spectrum disorder
  • learning disability
  • aphasia

However, you should base decisions on a patient's communication needs, rather than their diagnosis.

Information:

“If people cannot get information about their healthcare they understand, this can have a significant impact on their mental health and can lead to them missing doctor’s appointments or taking the wrong medication, putting them in danger." — Louise Ansari, national director at Healthwatch England

How the Accessible Information Standard applies to messaging

  1. Ask people to define their communication needs, including the format of messages they will be able to access and understand.
  2. Record their needs (not the disability, impairment or sensory loss) in a clear, unambiguous and standardised way.
  3. Flag these needs so they are easily visible to anyone accessing the record.
  4. Share these needs as part of existing data sharing processes, for example when referring a patient, so they will continue to receive accessible messages.
  5. Only send messages in a format that meets these needs, including any automatically generated messages.

Find out more about the accessible information standard.

Digital formats

Many people with communication needs use assistive technology to help them to understand written information.

For these people, sending messages by email [ADD LINK] or text message [ADD LINK] will mean they can use their preferred technology.

Alternative formats (accessible formats)

Some people will need messages to be sent in an alternative format. These are also called accessible formats.

People with a visual impairment may need:

  • large print
  • Braille
  • an audio file, sent digitally or on a CD-ROM

People with a learning disability or a low level of literacy may need:

  • easy read
  • Makaton

People who are Deaf or have hearing loss may need:

  • British Sign Language (BSL)
  • Makaton
  • textphone

People with co-ordination difficulties may need:

  • large print
  • audio
  • telephone

Find out more about alternative communication formats.

Use inclusive language

Use our content guide to create inclusive content about:

  • age
  • disabilities and conditions
  • sex, gender and sexuality
  • skin symptoms

The Learning disability and autism programme has also created guidance about how to talk about people with a learning disability or autism.

Translating message content into other languages

Make all possible efforts to meet patients' communication needs in languages other than English.

Ask patients to tell you their preferred written language. Record this preference in their health record and include this information when referring patients to other healthcare professionals.

Translate message content into a patient's preferred language whenever possible.

You should:

  • provide the original message to the translator in plain English
  • consider if audio-recorded spoken interpretation of information may be a better option if the person has a low level of literacy
  • consider if visual content like images and diagrams may be more helpful

Avoid Google Translate and other automated online translation services, as the translations could be poor quality.
