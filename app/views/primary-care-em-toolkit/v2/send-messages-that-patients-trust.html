Send messages that patients trust

To help patients act on the messages we send them, it’s important they can trust that messages are safe, secure and have genuinely been sent by the NHS.

Quality and relevance of messages

Spelling and grammatical errors or inaccuracies in a message could mean people don’t trust that it's genuine. To avoid this, you should:

  • follow the general principles in the NHS Content guide when writing messages so our messages have a consistent style
  • follow the National Cyber Security Centre guidance on SMS best practice to help make message content trustworthy
  • always get messages checked by someone else before you send them to check for spelling or grammatical errors and inaccuracies

Links

Do not use a third-party link shortening service to reduce the length of links, because:

  • your users cannot see where the link will take them
  • your link might stop working if there’s a service outage
  • you can no longer control where the redirect goes

Find out more about using links and URLs on GOV.UK Notify.

Sender names on text messages

The text address which appears on a text message in place of the sending telephone number is called a SenderID.

Using an appropriate SenderID can help build trust with message recipients.

You should use MEF-registered (official) SenderIDs.
