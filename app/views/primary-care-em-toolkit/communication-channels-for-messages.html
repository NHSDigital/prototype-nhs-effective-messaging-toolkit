Messaging patients

  • Communication channels for messages

Communication channels for messages

Most [message services] will only let you decide which communication channel to use to message an individual patient. They will have fixed channels for sending [messages to multiple patients].

Avoid sending the same message using multiple channels at the same time. This can overwhelm patients. It also increases costs for the NHS.

Send messages that patients can understand

If a patient with a disability, impairment or sensory loss has a recorded communication need, you must send messages in a format they can access. Find out more about sending accessible messages.

App messages

Sending a message through the NHS App is free and secure. It also means patients can manage all their healthcare information in one place. Find out more about the benefits of NHS App messaging.

Patients need to download the NHS App and turn on their notifications before they can receive messages through the app. Find resources to promote NHS App messaging, including example text message templates.

If a patient does not see an app message

If a patient does not see an app message notification or read the message, your [message service] will send the message by text message, email or letter.

Each [message service] will have a different channel send order and timescales. Speak to your [???] to find out more.

Keep app messages as short as possible

App messages might also be sent as text messages if they haven't been read on the NHS App, so you should keep messages as short as possible. Find out more about text message fragments.

Emails

Sending an email is free. It can be more convenient and accessible than a paper letter.

Patients may also prefer to have information emailed to them if they need to forward it to someone else, for example a fit note (sick note).

Find out how to make your emails more accessible.

Your email could end up in a patient's junk folder or they might not trust it is from the NHS. Find out more about sending messages that patients trust.

Text messages (SMS)

Sending text messages costs the NHS millions of pounds each year.

If you try to send a text message and it is not delivered to the patient, you will still be charged.

Regularly check that the mobile numbers you have for patients are up to date.

Text message fragments

Text messages are split into chunks called fragments.

  • 160 characters is 1 fragment.
  • 306 characters is 2 fragments.
  • 459 characters is 3 fragments.
  • Each additional 153 characters is another fragment.

Each fragment costs about 2 pence.

If you go 1 character over a fragment limit, you are charged for another fragment. For example:

  • 306 characters will be 2 fragments and cost about 4 pence
  • 307 characters will be 3 fragments and cost about 6 pence

How to sender fewer fragments

You should keep text messages as short as possible but make sure you include all the information the patient needs to know.

Following our content guide can help you to write messages clearly and concisely.

If you use templates for your messages, check the fragment length and remove any words that are not needed, for example COVID-19 guidance.

You can also reduce the number of fragments you send by:

  • only using spaces and line breaks where they are needed (spaces are 1 character and line breaks are 2 characters)
  • using widely known acronyms, for example 'A&E', rather than 'accident and emergency'
  • only using signs and symbols, like brackets and quote marks, if the message would be hard to understand without them

Find out more about how signs, symbols and accented characters can increase text message fragments.

Make sure everyone can understand your text messages

Reducing text message fragments is important. But it is more important that all patients can understand your messages.

You should:

  • avoid using acronyms not everyone will be familiar with, for example 'OTC' for 'over the counter'
  • avoid using abbreviations, for example 'Mon' for 'Monday', 'Feb' for February or 'appt' for 'appointment'

Find out more about sending accessible messages.

Look at our suggested wording for common messages, which have been tested with patients and are as short as possible.

Links in text messages

Not all patients with a mobile telephone number have a smartphone.

Give patients another way to respond if they are not able to follow the links in a text message. Find out more about reaching digitally excluded patients.

If you include links in a text message, do not use a [link shortening tool]. They are not accessible for patients using assistive technology.

Create a communications policy

Create a communications policy that outlines what types of messages will be sent using each channel.

This will make your messaging more consistent for patients, so they know what to expect. It will also help you budget for messaging costs.

NHS England has created a template for email and text message communications.
