Make standard messages accessible

Aim to make all the text messages, emails and app messages you send accessible to people who:

  • use assistive technology, like a screenreader
  • have a low level of literacy
  • have low numeracy
  • have a learning difficulty or autism

This will reduce the number of messages you need to send in accessible formats. These are also known as accessible formats.

Use our content guide to write messages that are:

  • clear and in in plain English, so someone with a reading age of 9 to 11 years can understand them
  • concise, with short sentences and paragraphs
  • in the active voice, not passive voice

Specific pages of the content guide tell you how to:

  • use words and phrases that patients understand
  • use formatting and punctuation
  • write numbers, measurements, dates and times
  • use links

The GOV.UK style guide also has a useful list of words to avoid, with plain English alternatives.

The messages you send might include links to your website. Find out how to make your website usable and accessible for patients.

How to send messages that meet the Accessible Information Standard

All organisations that provide NHS care are [legally] required to follow the Accessible Information Standard. The standard outlines how you must meet the information and communication support needs of people with a disability, impairment or sensory loss. This includes patients and service users, and their carers and parents.

Identifying who the Accessible Information Standard might apply to

You must ask anyone who uses your services and has a disability, impairment or sensory loss if they have any communication or information needs.

Patient codes that might indicate an additional communication need
  • Registered blind or partially sighted
  • Hearing loss
  • Hearing aid user
  • Registered deaf
  • Sensori-neural deafness
  • Autistic spectrum disorder
  • Learning disability
  • Aphasia

You must:

  • ask them to define their needs, including the format of messages they will be able to access and understand
  • record the needs in a clear, unambiguous and standardised way (record the needs, not the disability, impairment or sensory loss)
  • make sure the needs are flagged and easily visible to anyone accessing the record
  • only send messages to the patient in the agreed format, including any automatically generated messages
  • share the needs as part of existing data sharing processes, for example when referring a patient, so they will continue to receive accessible messages
