Decide what type of message to send

Most [message services] will only let you decide what time of message to send for individual messages. They will have a set priority order for [batch] messages.

Avoid sending the same message using multiple channels at the same time. This can overwhelm patients. It also increases costs for the NHS.

Send messages that patients can understand

If a patient has a recorded communication need, you must send messages in a format they can access. Find out more about sending accessible messages

App messages

Sending a message through the NHS App is free and secure. It also means patients can manage all their healthcare information in one place. Find out more about the benefits of NHS App messaging.

Patients need to download the NHS App and turn on their notifications before they can receive messages through the app. Find out more about the benefits of NHS App messaging.

If a patient does not see an app message

If a patient does not see an app message notification or read the message, your [message sending service] will send the message by text message, email or letter.

Each service will send the message again using different channels and timescales. Speak to your [???] to find out more.

Keep app messages as short as possible

As app messages might be sent as text messages, you should keep messages as short as possible. [ANCHOR LINK] Find out more about text message fragments.

Emails

Sending an email is free. It can be more convenient and accessible than a paper letter.

Example uses of email

  • Appointment confirmations when there is a lot of information the patient needs to know, like any specific things they need to do before the appointment.
  • Copies of letters, for example from a consultant.
  • Patient information leaflets

Your email could end up in a patient's junk folder or they might not trust it is from the NHS. Find out more about sending messages that patients trust.

Text messages (SMS)

Sending text messages costs the NHS millions of pounds each year.

Text messages should only be sent if:

  • the patient does not have the NHS App
  • you are requesting information that is needed quickly

Example uses of text messages

Urgent requests for the patient:

  • to send a photo to a clinician
  • to join a video consultation
  • to complete a form or send a document

Text message fragments

A fragment is 1 standard text message, around 160 characters long. Each fragment costs about 1.85p.

You should keep text messages as short as possible but make sure you include all the information the patient needs to know.

Following our content style guide can help you to write messages clearly and concisely.

If you use templates for your messages, check the fragment length and remove any words that are not needed.

Look at our suggested wording for common messages, which have been tested with patients and are as short as possible.
